mad-research

  • a chance to produce documentation as a product -- a designed object that would serve as legal evidence of inhabiting a piece of land (similar to lagos project)
  • in making this product, a chance to research and educate end users about data literacy
  • a chance to think about sustainability instead of top down designs
  • building an organizational model that would make our mission sustainable long term
  • not copying and pasting a first world technology and expecting it to work
in a country of 5,077 NGOs (and a population of 2.84 million)
we wanted to work in the ger districts, helping people learn the skills for mapping their own yards, to create documentation for when they wanted to register their parcels, but also to foster more informed grassroots design.
there were other great projects like Asia Foundation's work with Open Street Map to help UB's government map ger districts for city planning purposes
we found that people living in ger districts were actually implementing some of the city planning bureau's ideas, faster than the government itself! from greenhouses to district improvement
we knew our work would be to ]bridge the gap[ between what the government and NGOs were doing with data, and what citizen groups could do with data and taking design into their own hands. what kind of data do we need for this?
micro-data: meaningful data made for people, by people
surveys: getting people engaged
instead of having a poll or anonymous survey taker, train embedded people in the community
  • be specific in at least half of your survey - asking vague, or open ended questions can be intimidating for people who think you expect a so-called correct answer
  • but do offer space for people who want to go one step further
  • have survey takers be embedded within the community - so that these questions are not coming from an outsider but from community members
    • who represents the tone of the project, going into it?
    • what baseline design ideas do you need to question -- color? shapes? understandings of privacy?
    • what can you give back, in non monetary, non material ways?
    volunteers learned about collecting data, because it was their first time, they shared what they were doing openly and transparently with the surveyees.
    in 213 surveys, 70% of people mentioned that they were excited to have such a transparent process -- usually NGOs come in, take information, and they had no clue where the information they gave was going to
    and of course, we found all kinds of surprises about what format our documentation and maps of each household's land plot should take - from colors, to access to digital maps, to how directions were understood or given.
